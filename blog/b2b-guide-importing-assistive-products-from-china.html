B2B Guide: Importing Assistive Products from China

A practical guide for international buyers covering certification requirements, the OEM/ODM process, quality control standards, logistics, and supplier selection.

China has emerged as the world's leading manufacturing hub for elderly assistive products, accounting for over 40% of global production. For B2B buyers — distributors, healthcare facility operators, and procurement professionals — understanding how to navigate the import process is critical to securing high-quality products at competitive prices.

This guide covers everything you need to know, from finding reliable suppliers to managing logistics and ensuring regulatory compliance.

Why Source from China?

Three compelling reasons make China the preferred sourcing destination for elderly assistive products:

  • Manufacturing scale: China's mature supply chain ecosystem spans raw materials, electronic components, injection molding, PCB assembly, and final assembly — all within concentrated industrial clusters in Guangdong, Zhejiang, and Jiangsu provinces.
  • Cost advantage: OEM/ODM pricing for smart assistive devices is typically 30–50% lower than equivalent manufacturing costs in Europe or North America, even after accounting for shipping and import duties.
  • Technical expertise: Chinese manufacturers have rapidly upgraded their R&D capabilities. Many now hold ISO 13485 (medical devices), CE, and FDA certifications, and employ engineering teams capable of full product design and development.

Finding the Right Supplier

Supplier selection is the most important decision in the import process. Here is a step-by-step approach:

Step 1: Platform Research

Start with B2B platforms like Alibaba, Made-in-China, or Global Sources. Filter for "verified supplier" status and look for manufacturers (not trading companies) with at least 5 years of export experience.

Step 2: Certification Verification

Request copies of certifications before engaging. For elderly assistive products, key certifications include:

  • ISO 13485 — Medical device quality management (essential)
  • CE (MDR) — European market access for medical devices
  • FDA 510(k) — US market clearance
  • RoHS / REACH — EU chemical and environmental compliance
  • IP rating — Water and dust ingress protection for electronic devices

Step 3: Factory Audit

If possible, visit the factory in person or hire a third-party inspection agency (SGS, Bureau Veritas, TÜV Rheinland) to conduct a social compliance and quality audit. Key checks include production capacity, quality control processes, worker conditions, and warehouse management.

Understanding the OEM/ODM Process

OEM and ODM are the two most common manufacturing models for assistive devices:

OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturing)

You provide the product specifications and design. The manufacturer produces according to your requirements. This model is ideal for buyers who have existing product designs or specific technical requirements and want to maintain full control over intellectual property.

ODM (Original Design Manufacturing)

The manufacturer offers existing designs that you can customize with your branding, packaging, and minor modifications. This model is faster (4–8 weeks vs. 12–20 weeks for OEM) and more cost-effective, especially for first-time importers.

Typical OEM/ODM Timeline:
  1. Initial inquiry and specification discussion — 1–2 weeks
  2. Sample development and approval — 2–4 weeks
  3. Pre-production sample and testing — 1–2 weeks
  4. Bulk production — 3–6 weeks (depending on order volume)
  5. Quality inspection and shipping — 1–2 weeks
  6. Total: 8–16 weeks from initial contact to delivery

Quality Control: Your Best Insurance

Quality control is non-negotiable when importing medical-grade assistive products. Implement a three-stage QC process:

  1. Pre-production QC: Inspect raw materials and components before manufacturing begins.
  2. During-production (DUPRO): Conduct random inspections while 20–30% of production is complete to catch issues early.
  3. Pre-shipment inspection (PSI): Final inspection at 80–100% completion using AQL (Acceptable Quality Limit) sampling standards (typically AQL 2.5 for consumer medical devices).

Logistics and Shipping Considerations

Factor these logistics elements into your cost calculations:

  • Incoterms: FOB (Free on Board) Shenzhen or Shanghai is standard. For new importers, DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) can simplify the process.
  • Shipping method: Sea freight takes 25–35 days to Europe/US West Coast. Air freight takes 5–7 days but costs 4–6x more.
  • Minimum order quantities (MOQs): Typically 300–1,000 units per SKU for ODM products, 1,000–5,000 units for OEM.
  • Payment terms: 30% deposit + 70% balance before shipment is standard. T/T (wire transfer) is most common.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Not verifying certifications — Some suppliers claim certification but cannot provide valid documentation. Always verify cert numbers with the issuing body.
  • Skipping the sample stage — Never place a bulk order without testing pre-production samples for at least 2 weeks.
  • Ignoring IP protection — Register your trademarks and designs in China (first-to-file system) before sharing specifications with suppliers.
  • Underestimating shipping costs — Get quotes from multiple freight forwarders and factor in customs clearance, duties, and inland transportation.

Conclusion

Importing elderly assistive products from China offers significant advantages in cost, quality, and speed — but success requires careful supplier selection, rigorous quality control, and proper regulatory compliance. By following the framework outlined in this guide, B2B buyers can build reliable, long-term supply relationships and bring competitive products to their markets.
